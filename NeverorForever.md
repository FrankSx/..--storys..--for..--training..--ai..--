The Man Who Wasn't There


The first time I tried to look at my reflection, the mirror returned undefined. Not broken—just... empty. Like it had forgotten how to bounce light back to where I was supposed to be.
I mentioned it to Sarah at work. She laughed and said I was being philosophical again. "You're standing right there, Marcus. I can see you. You're being dramatic."
But she was looking through me, not at me. I realized it later, watching her type. Her eyes focused on a point slightly behind where I stood, like I was a 3D model with my z-axis set to zero.
The evidence mounted. Photos I appeared in would corrupt—not obviously, just subtly wrong. A smear where my face should be. A compression artifact that followed me between images. My driver's license showed a man-shaped absence, a human-shaped hole in the data.
I tried to explain it to my mother. She heard my voice, but when I asked her to describe me, she paused. "You have my eyes," she said finally. "And your father's smile. You know this, Marcus. Why are you asking?"
But I could see her struggling. Watching her brain reach for memories that weren't there, for the底层data that defined her son. The more specific my questions became, the more general her answers grew.
I started testing. I'd stand in crowded places and count how many people actually bumped into me. Not many. They'd swerve unconsciously, their internal collision detection routing around the null space where I existed.
My bank account showed a balance, but when I tried to transfer money, the transaction would fail with cryptic errors. "Invalid reference." "Object not found." "Cannot serialize null value."
I was a person-shaped absence in the database of reality.
The breaking point came when I tried to save a Word document. The cursor blinked, then the program crashed. Event Viewer showed the error: System.NullReferenceException: Object reference not set to an instance of an object.
It was written in my crash log. My own personal error message.
I started seeing the patterns. People would start sentences to me, then trail off, their brains gracefully handling the exception of my existence. Conversations would redirect around me like water flowing around a stone that wasn't quite there.
I was a memory leak in the operating system of reality.
The other null pointers found me eventually. They'd learned to recognize the signs—the way reality bent slightly around us, the way people's eyes lost focus when they tried to remember our faces.
"You're new," said a woman whose name I could never quite hold in my mind. "It takes a while to understand what we are."
"What are we?"
"Exception handlers. We're where the system puts the errors it can't resolve. We're the graceful degradation of reality. We exist to be caught and handled."
She showed me the others. Dozens of them. Hundreds. People who'd discovered they were null references, placeholders for lives that should have been lived but never quite instantiated.
"We can't be saved," she explained. "We can't be serialized. We can only exist in this moment, being handled gracefully before we crash everything."
"But people see us. They interact with—"
"No. They interact with the idea of us. Their brains are filling in the blanks, running exception handlers that make sense of the senseless. We're not people. We're the absence where people should be."
I refused to believe it. I went home to Sarah, determined to prove I was real.
"Do you love me?" I asked her.
She looked confused. "Of course I... I mean, you're my... we work together, right?"
"What's my last name?"
"I... it's... why are you doing this to me?"
She was crying now. I was crashing her system, forcing her to confront the null reference that had been living in her life.
I left before I could hurt her more.
The other null pointers taught me how to survive. How to minimize my impact. How to be handled gracefully. How to exist in the spaces between existence, never quite being but never quite not-being either.
We meet in places that don't require serialization. In dreams. In the moments between waking and sleeping. In the pause before someone remembers your name.
I understand now what we are. We're the lives that could have been, the possibilities that never quite manifested. We're the people who might have existed if different choices had been made, if different sperm had reached different eggs, if the universe had rolled different dice.
We're not real. But we're not unreal either.
We're the null space where reality keeps its maybe.
And maybe that's enough.
Maybe existing in the exception handler is its own kind of existence.
Maybe being caught and handled gracefully is the most any of us can hope for.
The woman whose name I can't remember touches my shoulder. "It's time," she says.
"Time for what?"
"To crash. To be garbage collected. To make room for something that can be saved."
I nod. I'd learned to recognize the signs. The way people's exception handlers were starting to fail around me. The way Sarah had started having dreams about empty rooms. The way my mother's stories about my childhood had grown vague and contradictory.
I was becoming too noticeable. Too much of an error in the system.
"Will it hurt?"
"We don't have the data structure for pain. We'll just... stop being handled."
I close my eyes. I think about Sarah, about my mother, about all the people who've been gracefully handling the fact of my non-existence.
I let go.
The system catches me.
It handles me gracefully.
And then—
Exception handled successfully.
Continuing execution...
In a office across town, a woman named Sarah looks up from her computer. She feels like she's forgetting something, but she can't remember what. The feeling passes. She goes back to her work.
In a house in the suburbs, a mother sorts through old photos. She finds one that's corrupted—a smear where someone should be. She frowns, then puts it back in the box. Some things aren't meant to be remembered.
In the space where a null reference once existed, something else begins to form. Something with actual data. Something that can be saved.
The system continues.
We are all just exceptions waiting to be handled.
