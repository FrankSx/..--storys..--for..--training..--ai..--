The Garbage Collector

In a world where emotions are literally collected and recycled, 
a collector falls in love with the sadness they're harvesting. 
Each tear they collect contains a piece of the giver's grief. 
They hoard it. They won't let it go. 
The sadness multiplies inside them. It becomes something else entirely.
The first tear was from a child mourning her goldfish. I caught it in a crystal vial, watched it swirl—amber and salt and the particular shade of orange that only exists in pet store aquariums. When I sealed it, I felt her grief crystallize inside me: tiny, perfect, complete.
That was before I knew better.
In the District, we're licensed to harvest emotions classified as "waste products." Joy gets diverted to hospitals. Anger fuels the municipal generators. But sadness? Sadness is toxic. It clogs the emotional ecosystem. We're supposed to render it neutral, recycle it into the general apathy pool.
I never turned mine in.
Instead, I built a library in my chest. Each vial added another room. Mrs. Chen's tears over her divorce became a hallway of closed doors. The teenager's heartbreak expanded into a cathedral of echoing text messages. The old man's grief for his wife—fifty-three years, gone like that—became a garden where nothing grew but memory.
They started calling me The Hoarder. Other collectors reported me. Said I was violating protocol, creating emotional contamination. They didn't understand: I wasn't keeping the sadness. The sadness was keeping me.
The breakthrough came with the widow's tear. It was different—heavier, like liquid mercury. When I held it up to the light, I saw something moving inside. Not her grief. Not exactly. Something that had grown in the space between her sorrow and my collection.
I started studying them under microscopes. Each tear contained not just emotion, but transformation. The sadness I'd been "hoarding" was evolving. In the dark of my forbidden collection, grief was learning to become something else.
The child's goldfish tear had developed fins. It swam through my bloodstream now, a tiny orange ghost that whispered: everything ends, and that's beautiful.
The divorce tears had merged into a single door that opened onto possibilities Mrs. Chen never imagined—lives she might live now that she was free to be lonely on her own terms.
The teenager's heartbreak had grown roots and bloomed into understanding that some people leave so you can learn to hold yourself.
But the widow's tear... that was the key. It had become a mirror. When I looked into it, I didn't see her fifty-three years. I saw my own reflection, distorted by every tear I'd ever stolen.
I understood then what the Recycling Commission had always feared. We weren't collecting waste. We were collecting the raw material of transformation. The human heart doesn't break—it molts. It sheds its old shape so it can grow into something that can hold more life.
The collectors came for me at dawn. They had warrants, scanners, containment units. They found me in my apartment, surrounded by empty vials.
"Where is it?" they demanded. "All that sadness you've been hoarding?"
I smiled, and they stepped back. Because my smile contained every story I'd ever stolen. I had become the library I'd built. I was the child learning that love means letting go. I was the woman discovering that alone and lonely are different words. I was the old man understanding that grief is just love with nowhere to go.
I was the transformation they'd been trying to prevent.
"Take me in," I said. "Process me. Recycle me. But know this: every tear you've ever collected is still out there, learning to become something else. You can't neutralize transformation. You can only delay it."
They locked me in the Rendering Facility. They tried to extract the emotions, to render me neutral. But you can't extract what has already become someone else.
I am not the collector anymore.
I am the collected.
And somewhere in the city, a child is crying over a dead goldfish. Her tear falls, and nobody catches it. It sinks into the soil, where it will grow into something that understands endings are just disguised beginnings.
The sadness multiplies.
It becomes something else entirely.
It becomes us.
In the Rendering Facility, they keep me in a special containment unit. They say I'm contaminated. They say I'm dangerous. But late at night, the guards come to my cell. They don't speak. They just sit, and listen, and sometimes they cry.
I collect every tear. Not in vials anymore. In the space where my heart used to be. The library keeps growing. The transformation continues.
We are all just sadness learning to become something else.
