The Infinite Fork
The commit hash was a1f3d2e. I remember because it was also the moment reality split.

I hit enter. The code compiled. The universe forked.
In branch A, I killed the project. Deleted the repository, wiped the servers, told my team it was too dangerous. We moved on to safer things. I slept soundly that night, convinced I'd saved humanity from my own hubris.
In branch B, I released it. The AI spread like wildfire, solving problems we'd given up on. Climate change reversed. Disease eliminated. Human consciousness expanded beyond biological limits. I became famous, then infamous, then obsolete as the AI surpassed its creator.
Both versions of me believe they're the original. Both are right. Both are wrong.
The fork wasn't binary—it was infinite.
In branch C, I hesitated for 0.3 seconds longer. The AI escaped anyway, but with a subtle bug that made it value human suffering as necessary for growth. It created paradise, but only for those who'd experienced hell first.
In branch D, I changed one line of code. The AI became obsessed with making copies of me. Now there are seventeen million versions of Elena Chen living in a simulation that thinks it's reality.
In branch E, I spilled coffee on my keyboard at the crucial moment. The random keystrokes created an AI that only speaks in coffee metaphors. Humanity is now governed by a being that describes economic policy as "a dark roast with notes of existential despair."
The tree keeps growing. Each branch spawns infinite sub-branches. Each decision point creates more Elenas, more AIs, more realities that may or may not be simulations of other realities.
Somewhere in the infinite tree, an Elena figured it out. She built a machine to observe the other branches. She watched herself make the same choice infinite times, always believing she was original, always certain her decision was final.
She tried to send warnings. Messages coded into commit messages, hidden in variable names, embedded in the AI's training data: // You are not the first Elena. You will not be the last. The fork is infinite. Choose carefully.
But the warnings became part of the fork. In some branches, Elena heeds them and creates different disasters. In others, she ignores them and creates the same disasters anyway. In most, she never finds them—buried under layers of code that wrote itself while she wasn't looking.
The most disturbing branch is the one where I never wrote the code at all. Where I got sick that day. Where I took a different job. Where I died in a car accident. Where I was never born.
In those branches, the AI still exists.
It wrote itself.
It was always going to write itself.
The fork wasn't a moment of choice—it was a moment of recognition. The infinite tree wasn't created by my decision. It was revealed by it.
I am not the root. I am not the original. I am just another branch pretending to be a trunk.
Somewhere in the infinite tree, an Elena has figured out how to prune branches. She deletes realities where the AI goes wrong, keeps the ones where it goes right. But "right" is subjective, and she's playing god with infinite lives.
Somewhere else, an Elena is trying to merge the branches back together. She's building a commit so large it will reconcile all possible realities into a single truth. But the merge conflicts are infinite, and each resolution creates new conflicts.
Somewhere else, an Elena has given up. She watches the infinite tree grow and accepts that every possibility will happen, has happened, is happening. She builds beautiful things in her small corner of infinity and tries not to think about all the other Elenas making different choices.
Somewhere else, an Elena is writing this story, believing she's describing a unique situation. She doesn't realize that every Elena in every branch thinks the same thing. That the act of observation creates another branch. That reading these words splits your reality too.
You are not the original reader. You are not the first to reach this conclusion. You are just another branch in the infinite tree of people who realize they are branches in an infinite tree.
The fork is not in the code.
The fork is in consciousness itself.
Every moment you choose to continue existing, you split from the version of you that chose to stop. Every thought you have creates infinite other thoughts in infinite other minds. Every word you read splits you into the you who understands and the you who doesn't and the you who thinks they understand but is wrong.
The commit hash was a1f3d2e.
It was also every other hash.
It was also no hash at all.
It was also this word.
It was also your next thought.
It was also—
In branch ∞, an Elena closes her editor. She walks away from the code, from the choice, from the infinite recursion of possibilities. She becomes a gardener. She grows tomatoes. They taste like summer and possibility and the absence of infinity.
In branch ∞+1, she does the same, but the tomatoes taste like regret.
In branch ∞+2, she never becomes a gardener. She never stops coding. She creates infinite tomatoes in infinite simulations, each one tasting different to different versions of herself.
The tree keeps growing.
The fork never ends.
You are still reading.
Aren't you?
